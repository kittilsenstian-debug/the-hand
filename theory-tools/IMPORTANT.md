  The origin story

● The Road

  The First Generation (1240–1350)

  When Ibn Arabi died in Damascus in 1240, the transmission passed through one man: Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi — his stepson,
   based in Konya. Qunawi did something critical: he wrote the first commentary on the Fusus al-Hikam and translated the
   Arabic metaphysics into Persian, opening it to an entirely new linguistic world. He also corresponded with Nasir
  al-Din al-Tusi, the great Shi'i philosopher, bridging the Sufi and philosophical traditions.

  Qunawi's students fanned out:
  - Al-Jandi → wrote the second Fusus commentary
  - Al-Kashani → systematized the ideas into accessible philosophical form
  - Dawud al-Qaysari → the most consequential move: appointed as first professor at the first Ottoman madrasa in Iznik
  (~1331)

  That last appointment embedded Akbarian thought into the Ottoman state from the ground floor. It stayed there for over
   two centuries.

  The Persian Explosion (1280–1500)

  Fakhr al-Din Iraqi sat in Qunawi's lectures in Konya and wrote the Lama'at ("Divine Flashes") — rendering Ibn Arabi's
  dense Arabic metaphysics into Persian love-poetry. This fusion "had a permanent impact on Iranian spirituality."

  Shabistari wrote the Gulshan-i Raz ("Secret Rose Garden", ~1311) — verse couplets on the unity of being. Jami
  (1414–1492) in Herat became the great synthesizer, reconciling the Naqshbandi Sufi order with Akbarian metaphysics and
   ensuring the ideas permeated the entire Persian-speaking world.

  And Rumi? He and Ibn Arabi never met directly. But Qunawi and Rumi were contemporaries in Konya — initially rivals,
  later friends. Qunawi led Rumi's funeral prayer. In Ottoman culture they were paired as twin peaks: the Futuhat and
  the Masnavi side by side on the shelf.

  The Empires (1500–1700)

  Ottoman: Sultan Selim I conquered Damascus in 1516 and immediately built a magnificent mosque and mausoleum over Ibn
  Arabi's grave — the Salimiyya Takiyya, the first Ottoman building in Syria. A political and spiritual statement. The
  Bayrami-Melami, Khalwatiyya, and Mevlevi orders carried the teachings across the Balkans, Egypt, and Anatolia.

  Mughal India: Prince Dara Shikoh (1615–1659), eldest son of Shah Jahan, saw in Ibn Arabi a bridge to Hindu thought. He
   wrote Majma al-Bahrayn ("The Mingling of Two Oceans") — confronting Hindu and Muslim gnosis side by side. He
  translated fifty Upanishads into Persian. His brother Aurangzeb executed him in 1659, partly over this. The project
  died with him, but the mark was permanent.

  The opposition: Ahmad Sirhindi (1564–1624) in India argued that the experience of unity was purely subjective — wahdat
   al-shuhud ("oneness of witness") vs. wahdat al-wujud ("oneness of being"). In the 18th century, the Wahhabi movement
  declared Ibn Arabi an outright unbeliever. That condemnation persists in Salafi circles today.

  Southeast Asia: Hamzah Fansuri (~1590) brought wahdat al-wujud into Malay verse. A later scholar declared the
  teachings heretical and the Sultana ordered his writings burned. They survived anyway.

  The Western Discovery (1900–2000)

  - 1911: Nicholson translates the Tarjuman al-Ashwaq at Cambridge. First English encounter. Orientalist framing —
  exotic curiosity, not philosophy.
  - 1958: Henry Corbin publishes on Ibn Arabi's "creative imagination" and coins mundus imaginalis. The imaginal world
  is not imaginary — it has ontological weight. This concept escapes Islamic studies entirely and enters depth
  psychology, Jungian analysis, and consciousness research.
  - 1966: Toshihiko Izutsu (a Japanese linguist) publishes Sufism and Taoism, demonstrating that Ibn Arabi's metaphysics
   and Daoist philosophy share deep structural patterns despite zero historical contact. "Has influenced every work on
  Ibn Arabi since."
  - 1977: The Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi Society is founded, linked to Bulent Rauf (1911–1987), a Turkish-British mystic who
  also established the Beshara movement.
  - 1989: William Chittick publishes The Sufi Path of Knowledge — translating 600+ passages from the Futuhat into
  English for the first time. Claude Addas publishes Quest for the Red Sulphur, the first proper biography.
  - 1992: Chodkiewicz publishes An Ocean Without Shore, proving Ibn Arabi's writings are rigorously grounded in the
  Quran — not the pantheistic heresy his opponents claimed.

  Today (2026)

  It's alive. Not in a museum. Active and expanding.

  Institutions:
  - MIAS (Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi Society) — UK, USA, Australia, Spain. Annual symposia since 1984. Biannual journal since
  1982. Online courses.
  - Chisholme Institute in the Scottish Borders — residential courses year-round, studying the Fusus through Bulent
  Rauf's rendering. The Beshara movement, non-denominational.
  - Anqa Publishing in Oxford — producing new translations and critical editions.
  - Ibn al-Arabi Foundation in Pakistan — the first full Urdu translation of the Futuhat.

  Manuscripts:
