### Step-by-Step Protocol

**Day 0: Setup**

1. Mix sediment: 80% fine sand + 10% iron filings + 10% MnO2 by volume. The iron and manganese create a redox-active layer — they oxidize in contact with oxygenated water and reduce in anoxic conditions, establishing a chemical domain wall.

2. Layer sediment 3-5 cm deep in the glass dish. Tamp down gently to remove large air pockets but don't compact hard — the sediment needs to be permeable.

3. Fill with saltwater (35 g/L) to 5 cm above sediment surface. The water column should be about 2x the sediment depth.

4. Place aquarium heater horizontally on the bottom of the dish, BELOW the sediment layer (or against one side if the dish is too shallow). Set to 35-40 degrees C. This creates a temperature gradient: hot below, cooler water above.

5. Cover loosely (cling wrap with holes for gas exchange). Place in a stable location away from vibration.

6. Start time-lapse camera pointed at the sediment surface from above.

**Day 1-3: Equilibration**

The temperature gradient establishes. You should see gentle convection in the water column (test with a drop of food coloring). The sediment surface develops a thin oxidized layer (rusty orange from iron, dark brown from MnO2) over the anoxic interior (grey/black).

**Day 3-7: Pattern Formation (if it works)**

Watch the sediment surface. Convective cells in the water column should imprint on the sediment through differential mineral deposition:
- Upwelling zones: warm, slightly anoxic water rises -> reduced minerals at surface -> dark patches
- Downwelling zones: cool, oxygenated water sinks -> oxidized minerals -> light patches
- The boundary between up and down zones: the domain wall

If hexagonal cells form, measure:
- Cell diameter (framework predicts ~2x sediment depth)
- Vertex connectivity (framework predicts degree-3, i.e. three edges meeting at each vertex, 120 degree angles)
- Regularity (framework predicts high regularity if n >= 2; random if n < 2)

**Day 7-21: Maturation**

If the pattern develops, let it mature. The mineral boundaries should sharpen over time as the redox chemistry accumulates iron oxides at cell boundaries and reduced iron sulfides (dark) at cell centers (or vice versa depending on the flow direction).
