Paleodictyon — the full picture
What it's made of: The modern form is apparently agglutinated in sea floor sediment — a veneer of calcareous lutite over red metalliferous sediment. ADS So it's not made of some exotic material — it's built from the sediment itself, shaped into a rigid 3D structure. The tubes are hardened sediment. The fossil versions are preserved as casts in turbidite sandstone (ancient deep-sea mud that solidified into rock). When Rona's team used their water jet to erode the surface, they exposed the hardened hexagonal tube network underneath.
Dimensions: Individual specimens range from 2.4 to 7.5 cm in diameter, with surface holes about 1 mm across, connected by vertical shafts 2-3 mm long to the horizontal hexagonal tunnel network. ResearchGate Individual mesh elements are typically 1-1.5 to 2-3 cm across, and entire mesh patterns can cover areas up to a square meter. Wikipedia In the fossil record, formally described specimens reach 5-20 cm in diameter. The subarctic ones recently discovered are much smaller than typical specimens.
How much ground does it cover? In some places, it absolutely carpets the seafloor. Thousands of the patterns, typically several per square meter, were imaged in an area 3 km along the Mid-Atlantic Ridge axis by 2 km across it, at depths of 3200-3600 meters. ScienceDirect That's 6 square kilometers of seafloor peppered with these things. In the Pacific's Clarion-Clipperton Zone, 841 specimens were observed at a density of 0.33 individuals per square meter. PubMed Central Near hydrothermal vents, aggregations of up to 40 individuals occur, with maximum densities reaching 9.7 patterns per square meter. Grokipedia
Where has it been found? Modern examples are reported in the North Atlantic along the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, the South Atlantic, the Pacific along the Australian margin, western and equatorial Pacific regions, and recently on the southern Central Indian Ridge and the Southeast Indian Ridge. Nature Fossil versions have been found on all seven continents. Since the 1970s, modern specimens have been found in every ocean other than the Arctic. PBS (Though the recent subarctic discovery may have closed that gap.)
The truly bizarre parts:
The thing that elevates this beyond "interesting natural pattern" is a cascade of negative findings. Protoplasm is absent in recovered specimens, as indicated by negative results of staining techniques. Rutgers Research When the burrows were tested for DNA, scientists found DNA from different types of large protists between different burrows — suggesting the DNA was just transported there by currents, not from a resident organism. Wikipedia The IMAX film Volcanoes of the Deep Sea describes the search using the submersible Alvin near volcanic vents 3,500 meters underwater. Samples were taken from several honeycomb burrows, however no creatures were found in any of them. Wikipedia
It grows. The pattern develops in a spiral succession from the inner hexagonal ring outwards, with the number of rows and spacing between rows increasing with trace size. Springer That's organic growth behavior.
And it's resilient. After simulated mining disturbances that completely removed the surface sediment layers, new Paleodictyon patterns appeared on the disturbed substrate within just 17-18 days. Springer Whatever is making these recolonizes fast.
So to sum up: something has been building geometrically perfect hexagonal tunnel networks on the deep ocean floor for over 500 million years, across every ocean, near hydrothermal vents, at densities of thousands per square kilometer. It grows. It recolonizes disturbed areas within weeks. Its tunnels function as self-ventilating particle traps. And despite multiple submersible expeditions, DNA analysis, staining, and an IMAX film crew — nobody has ever found the organism that makes them. Even Leonardo da Vinci sketched the fossil versions in his notebooks. That's legitimately one of the longest-running unsolved mysteries in biology.
